How health insurance coverage pattern varies across time and states?¶

As my proposal suggest (review my proposal here), I am interested in three aspects of health insurance coverage: the variation arcoss time and locations, comparison between demographic data pattern and business data, and the relationship with some socioeconomic indicators. In this report, I would like to explore them in eight type of charts. First, I would explore the holistic situation of U.S., and then take four states as examples to roughly examine the influence of Ombama care.

Story about U.S.¶

Dempgraphic Pattern vs Business Pattern¶

The following three Cartograms and three Choropleth maps compare the total health insurance coverage reported by American Community Survey with the number of benefit plans per capita offered in each states in 2014 - 2016. In order to make the demographic data and business data comparable, I use the same source of total population data to calculate counting rates.
Apparently, there are differences between these two patterns. In terms of total health insurance coverage, the northeastern part of U.S. performs better while the business plans stand out in the mid-north and northwestern part of U.S. Since the report in ACS does not distinguish between private and public insurance sources, it is reasonable to speculate that the public insurance might did a much better job in northeastern states which leads to a fair total covarage in these states. In addition, if we compare the temporal pattern, we could tell that the total health insurance coverage is increasing across years. However, the number of business plans per capitia has a decrease from 2015 to 2016.

However, the population pyramid implying covered population has a slim waist, which indicates that there is few labor force population under the coverage of health insurance. This situation makes some sense here since labor population might be risk-loving rather than risk-averse. Therefore, they are more willing to invest money in other places. However, if we assume that any labor population who are affiliated to any organization should be coveraged by health insurance, there might be a lot of labor population some population who are employed or do not satisfy this requirement.\ But from these charts, we cannot tell the accurate differences between coverage rates by age groups and gender. There are some common trends before and after 2010 in these states: a sharp increase in total health insurance coverage appeared around 2013 and around 2016 it smooths down. So, overall, the total health insurance coverage improved since then. The medicaid also increases after 2010. However, the employer insurance decreases after 2010, which corresponds to the one of the major cons of Obama Care. Because Many businesses found it more cost-effective to pay the penalty and let their employees purchase insurance plans on the exchanges. Other small businesses find they can get better plans through the state-run exchanges.\ Besides that, We can also see the differences: Texas has the lowest total coverage rate but its employer insurance coverage is consistent with California. California has the sharpest increase in total health insurance coverage and medicaid.

Explanation on Theming:

  • color palette: use blues for health coverage related elements while use yellows for total population. In terms of gender, I use a lighter and a darker color to distinguish. The shade of blue colors represents the proportion of coverage rate for different care types(in general, employer insurance covered most people).

  • I use a lot of ggplot2 style theming elements. The reference source is here: https://github.com/vega/vega-themes/blob/master/src/theme-ggplot2.ts but I also set up the title to be fantasy font with size 18 , and subtitle to be droid serif with size 15. Both colors are '#2a3f5f'.

Encodings I used:

  • color hue: for coverage type
  • color saturation: for proportion of coverage type/ change in years
  • shape: for ses indicators
  • length: for number of population, coverage rate
  • position: for genders in population pyramids
  • size: before 2010 and after 2010

stevens scale types I used:

  • nomial: coverage type, gender
  • ordinal: year
  • ratio: coverage rate etc.
